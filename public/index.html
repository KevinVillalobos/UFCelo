▶ How ELO rankings work

What is ELO?
ELO is a relative rating system originally created for chess. Every fighter starts at 1500. Each fight transfers ELO points between the two fighters — the winner gains points, the loser loses them. The amount transferred depends on how surprising the result was: beating a much higher-rated opponent earns more than beating a lower-rated one.

Win probability formula
P(A wins) = 1 / (1 + 10(ELO_B − ELO_A)/400)
A 200-point gap gives ~76% win probability; a 400-point gap gives ~91%.

Variable K-factor
K controls how many points change hands. This system uses a variable K:
• New fighters (<5 fights): K is highest — ratings settle quickly
• Experienced fighters (15+ fights): K is lower — stable, hard-earned ratings
Each division also has a multiplier (Heavyweight = 1.0×, Welterweight = 0.75×, etc.)

Method & round weights
Not all wins are equal. A first-round KO earns more than a split decision:
• KO/TKO Rd 1: 1.40× · KO/TKO Rd 2: 1.25× · KO/TKO Rd 3+: 1.10×
• Submission: 1.20–1.25× · Unanimous decision: 1.05× · Majority: 0.90× · Split: 0.80×
Title fights add an additional 1.20× multiplier.

Streak multipliers
Win streaks and loss streaks amplify K: a 3-win streak adds 1.15×, a 3-loss streak adds 1.25× to the loser's drop.

Inactivity decay
Fighters inactive for more than a few months slowly drift back toward 1500 — preventing retired fighters from holding top spots indefinitely.
